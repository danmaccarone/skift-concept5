'Desperate' to Travel, but Rising Costs Point to 'Uneven' Summer

Skift Take

Summer 2026 demand is holding but bifurcating sharply. High-income households are spending more; lower-income travelers are scaling back. The structural dynamic isn't a temporary blip — it's a preview of how AI-mediated booking and persistent inflation will stratify travel demand for the next two to three years. Revenue strategies calibrated for broad-market volume will underperform.

Higher energy costs are weighing on Americans, but surveys and reports suggest there's still plenty of demand for summer travel. AAA expects record travel this Memorial Day, which typically marks the start of summer vacation. Bank of America is tracking resilient travel spending among U.S. consumers, especially those in high-income households.[1]

→
Reinforces our Q1 2026 view on K-shaped travel demand. The affordability gap is widening faster than our base case projected — upper-upscale outperformance is likely to extend into Q3.

AAA Projects Record Memorial Day Travel

AAA forecasts 200,000 more travelers between May 21 and 25 compared to last year, making 2026 the busiest Memorial Day on record. This year's forecast expects 39.1 million people to travel by car — a 0.4% increase from last year despite a roughly 40% hike in fuel prices to more than $4.50 per gallon on average.[2]

Skepticism Trigger
"39.1 million travelers — busiest Memorial Day on record"
Credible
AAA methodology counts travelers within a 50-mile radius, which inflates totals. The directional signal is sound, but precise record framing deserves caution. Watch for AAA post-weekend actuals — historically within 3–5% of forecast.

K-Shaped Travel Trends Continue This Summer

The Bank of America Institute expects summer travel to look "resilient, but uneven" as K-shaped economic trends continue into the travel season. Lower-income households are more likely to have cut down travel-related spending this year, and nearly 40% say they have no travel plans looking forward.[3]

Meanwhile, middle- and higher-income households — many of which received larger tax refunds in 2026 — are dropping more money on airlines, lodging, and cruises. "When we have looked in the Bank of America data at the recipients of higher tax refunds this year than last year, one of the areas where we've seen a pickup in spending has been around travel," said David Tinsley, senior economist at the Bank of America Institute.[3]

Americans 'Desperate' for Vacations

A survey from Allianz Partners found 70% of Americans plan to travel this summer, despite rising travel costs. The majority (85%) — and especially women (90%) and Gen Xers (91%) — say they are "desperate" for a vacation this year. Meanwhile, 79% are concerned about rising travel costs.[4]

Travelers 'Thoughtful' With Budgets

American summer travel enthusiasm remains high despite rising prices. The average U.S. trip budget is just over $3,500, and nearly half of travelers expect to spend more than last summer. 72% plan to take at least one trip between June and September.[5]

Search Data Tracks Interest in Destination Dupes

Expedia in April said its data is tracking more demand for "destination dupes" to address rising airfare prices. Lodging searches for Philadelphia are up 80% from last year, while Fort Lauderdale is up 40%.[6]

Skepticism Trigger
"29% of Americans used AI tools to plan or book travel"
Early Signal
This figure comes from a single survey. "Used AI tools" may range from a ChatGPT itinerary query to an actual AI-mediated booking. Worth watching across multiple data sources before treating as a structural shift metric.
Skift Take
The headline demand numbers look good. But the distribution of that demand is the story: upscale and luxury have structural tailwinds while mid-scale faces a compressed summer. Revenue strategies built for broad-market volume are running into a two-speed market — and the gap is widening.
