Skift Take

The K-shaped recovery everyone talked about in 2021 has hardened into a K-shaped market structure. High-income households ($150K+) are spending 18% more on travel than 2019; households under $75K are spending 12% less. This isn't cyclical — it's a reconfiguration of who travels, where they go, and what they're willing to pay. If your revenue model assumes broad-market volume growth, this report will force a rewrite.

Executive Summary

Travel spending in 2026 is bifurcating along income lines faster than any time since Skift Research began tracking consumer behavior in 2012. High-income households are not just traveling more — they're traveling differently, with longer booking windows, higher willingness to pay, and stronger brand loyalty.

+18%
Travel spending growth among households earning $150K+ vs 2019 baseline — driven by international trips, premium lodging, and experiential add-ons.
-12%
Travel spending decline among households under $75K vs 2019 — shorter trips, budget lodging concentration, and deferred big-ticket purchases.

The wedge between these cohorts isn't narrowing. Inflation-adjusted lodging costs are up 23% since 2019, while median household income growth has lagged at 11%. For lower-income travelers, the affordability gap isn't temporary — it's structural.

[ Chart: Travel Spending by Income Quintile, 2019–2026 ]

This report provides segment-level forecasts for lodging, airlines, car rental, cruises, and attractions through Q4 2026. We've stratified all data by five household income tiers, allowing revenue managers to model demand scenarios based on their actual customer mix.

Key Finding: Lodging Segment Divergence

Upper-upscale and luxury hotels are reporting RevPAR growth of 8–12% year-over-year. Mid-scale and economy properties are down 3–7%. The gap is widening as high-income travelers trade up and budget travelers reduce trip frequency.

[ Chart: RevPAR Growth by Lodging Segment, Q1 2024 – Q1 2026 ]

Our forecast models three scenarios through Q4 2026 based on macroeconomic assumptions. In all three, upper-upscale maintains positive growth. Mid-scale returns to flat or positive growth only in the optimistic scenario where fuel prices drop below $4/gallon and the Federal Reserve cuts rates twice.

Methodology

This report synthesizes data from four primary sources: Bank of America aggregated spending data (anonymized, 12M+ cardholders), Skift proprietary surveys (n=8,200 U.S. travelers, fielded Feb–Mar 2026), STR lodging performance data, and Airlines for America passenger metrics. Income stratification uses IRS-adjusted household income bands. Full methodology and sample composition details are available in the appendix.
